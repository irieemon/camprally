# Email 3 — Day 5: Personal Story / Trust Builder

**Subject:** The night I learned the most important camping lesson

---

Hey,

I want to tell you about a night in the Adirondacks.

It was November. First real cold snap of the season. I'd been camping 20+ times by then — felt like I knew what I was doing. Set up camp in fading light, got the fire going, had dinner, was feeling good.

Then around 11pm, the temperature dropped fast. I had a 30°F sleeping bag and a 3-season pad. I'd been fine in those conditions before.

I was not fine that night.

I won't bore you with the details — you can probably imagine. But I lay there for hours, shivering, eating trail mix at 2am for the calories, trying to warm up with absolutely no success. Watched the fire die because I was too cold to care about rebuilding it.

First light couldn't come fast enough.

Here's what I learned: **the sleeping bag rating isn't the temperature you'll be comfortable at — it's the temperature you'll survive at.** There's a big difference.

The second lesson: I was cold because I was on a thin foam pad. Not because my sleeping bag was bad. Ground steals your body heat relentlessly. I'd always thought the bag was doing the insulation work. It isn't. The pad is.

I've never made that mistake since.

---

That trip changed how I think about gear planning. Not around the tent or the stove or the cookware — around the sleep system. Bag + pad, both rated appropriately, both actually good.

Anyway. I tell you this because it's the kind of thing I wish someone had told me before my third trip, not after my fifteenth.

If you're planning a spring trip — this is the perfect time of year to go. Nights are still cold (30–40°F in a lot of spots), but the days are beautiful, the bugs aren't out yet, and the forests are starting to wake up.

Pack the extra layer. Check your pad's R-value. You'll have a better time than I did that night in the Adirondacks.

— Lazlo
